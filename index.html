About This Dashboard

A state of exception — whether called a state of emergency, martial law, or emergency decree — is a legal mechanism by which governments suspend ordinary constitutional protections and concentrate power in the executive. While sometimes justified by genuine crises, emergency powers are frequently abused to suppress dissent, circumvent legislative oversight, and entrench authoritarian rule. Many countries have remained under continuous emergency for years or decades.

The Institute for the Study of States of Exception (ISSE) monitors the global landscape of emergency powers to promote accountability, informed public dialogue, and evidence-based scholarship. This dashboard provides a real-time view of countries currently operating under declared or de facto states of emergency, drawing on multiple automated data sources and manual verification.

Methodology & Data Sources

This dashboard combines data from multiple automated sources, cross-referenced and verified through manual overrides, to track active states of emergency worldwide.

Data Sources:

  • Wikipedia — Active states of emergency and martial law declarations scraped from the State of emergency and Martial law articles via the MediaWiki API. This serves as the primary baseline.
  • ICCPR Article 4(3) Derogations — Formal notifications by states parties to the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights declaring derogations from treaty obligations during emergencies. Sourced from the UN Treaty Collection.
  • GDACS — Active disaster alerts (earthquakes, floods, cyclones, etc.) from the Global Disaster Alert and Coordination System. Used to corroborate disaster-related emergencies, not as a standalone source.
  • ReliefWeb API — Humanitarian reports and ongoing disaster tracking from OCHA/ReliefWeb. Used to corroborate disaster-related emergencies.
  • GDELT DOC 2.0 — Global news monitoring for recent articles mentioning emergency-related terms. Used to confirm and boost existing entries.
  • Curated Overrides — Hand-verified corrections and additions maintained by ISSE researchers to mark expired declarations, confirm active ones, and add entries not captured by automated scrapers.

Verification Status: Each emergency is assigned a verification level — Verified (confirmed by multiple sources or manual review, ≥90%), Likely Active (strong evidence but not fully confirmed, 75–89%), or Unconfirmed (single-source or aging data, 50–74%). Only entries meeting the 50% threshold are displayed.

Emergency Types: Entries are automatically classified into five categories — Disaster, Public Health, Conflict/Security, Migration, and Governance — using keyword matching against titles and descriptions.

Update Frequency: The data pipeline runs automatically every 6 hours via GitHub Actions.

Limitations: This dashboard relies on automated data collection and may miss emergencies not covered by Wikipedia or English-language news sources. Classification is keyword-based and may occasionally miscategorize entries. Emergency start dates may be approximate. ICCPR derogation notifications may remain on file after an emergency has lapsed; we use manual overrides to correct for this.
